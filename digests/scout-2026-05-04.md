### 1. [Jailbreaking Vision-Language Models Through the Visual Modality](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.00583)

**Score: 8/10** · Novelty: notable · Watchlist: Strong match for AI safety / red-teaming watchlist. The cross-modality alignment gap finding is directly relevant to anyone working on VLM safety post-training or evaluating frontier model robustness.  
*Authors: Aharon Azulay, Jan Dubiński, Zhuoyun Li, Atharv Mittal, Yossi Gandelsman*  
*Categories: cs.AI, cs.CV, cs.LG*

**Triage:** 8/10 — Directly relevant to AI safety and red-teaming. Exposes a concrete, underexplored alignment gap in frontier VLMs (including Claude) with novel attack taxonomy and preliminary mitigation/interpretability results.

**TL;DR**
- Introduces four novel visual-modality jailbreak attacks on VLMs that exploit the gap between text-based safety training and visually-conveyed harmful intent.
- Visual cipher attack achieves 40.9% ASR on Claude-Haiku-4.5 vs. 10.7% for equivalent text cipher, demonstrating a measurable cross-modality alignment gap.
- Findings argue that robust VLM safety post-training must treat vision as a first-class target, not a secondary concern.

**Motivation**  
Current VLM safety alignment is predominantly trained on text-based harmful content, leaving the visual modality as an underexplored attack surface. As VLMs become more capable and widely deployed, this gap poses a real-world safety risk.

**Hypothesis**  
Text-based safety training does not automatically generalize to harmful intent conveyed through visual channels, meaning VLMs can be jailbroken via carefully crafted images even when equivalent text prompts are blocked.

**Methodology**  
Four attack types are designed: (1) visual cipher (harmful instructions encoded as symbol sequences with a legend), (2) object substitution (harmful objects replaced with benign stand-ins), (3) text-in-image substitution (harmful text replaced with benign words while visual context preserves meaning), and (4) visual analogy puzzles requiring inference of a prohibited concept. Attacks are evaluated across six frontier VLMs with attack success rate (ASR) as the primary metric, plus preliminary interpretability and mitigation experiments.

**Results**  
Visual attacks consistently outperform text-equivalent baselines across all six models. The visual cipher achieves 40.9% ASR on Claude-Haiku-4.5 vs. 10.7% for the text cipher — a ~4× gap. Preliminary interpretability and mitigation results provide early mechanistic insight into why the gap exists and how it might be closed.

**Interpretation**  
The results confirm a systematic cross-modality alignment gap: safety guardrails learned from text do not transfer to visually-encoded harmful intent. This is not a model-specific quirk but a broad pattern across frontier VLMs, suggesting a structural deficiency in current safety post-training pipelines.

**Context**  
Builds on prior work in text-based jailbreaks (cipher attacks, prompt injection) and adversarial image perturbations for VLMs, but shifts focus to semantically meaningful visual manipulations rather than pixel-level noise. Complements growing literature on multimodal safety evaluation.

**Limitations**  
Evaluation is limited to six models and ASR as the primary metric; no user study or real-world harm assessment. Interpretability and mitigation results are described as "preliminary," limiting actionable conclusions. Attack designs are relatively manual/structured, and it's unclear how they scale or generalize to more diverse harmful content categories.

**Why it matters to you**  
Highly relevant to AI safety researchers focused on alignment robustness, red-teaming, and multimodal model safety. The inclusion of Claude models and the cross-model generality of findings make this directly actionable for safety teams at frontier labs.

### 2. [Position: agentic AI orchestration should be Bayes-consistent](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.00742)

**Score: 7/10** · Novelty: notable · Watchlist: Relevant to agent architecture design and safe agent decision-making. Worth reading for researchers designing orchestration layers for multi-agent or tool-using systems, especially those concerned with calibration and principled uncertainty handling.  
*Authors: Theodore Papamarkou, Pierre Alquier, Matthias Bauer, Wray Buntine, Andrew Davison, Gintare Karolina Dziugaite, Maurizio Filippone, Andrew Y. K. Foong, Vincent Fortuin, Dimitris Fouskakis, Jes Frellsen, Eyke Hüllermeier, Theofanis Karaletsos, Mohammad Emtiyaz Khan, Nikita Kotelevskii, Salem Lahlou, Yingzhen Li, Fang Liu, Clare Lyle, Thomas Möllenhoff, Konstantina Palla, Maxim Panov, Yusuf Sale, Kajetan Schweighofer, Artem Shelmanov, Siddharth Swaroop, Martin Trapp, Willem Waegeman, Andrew Gordon Wilson, Alexey Zaytsev*  
*Categories: cs.AI, cs.LG, stat.ML*

**Triage:** 7

**TL;DR**
- Position paper arguing that the orchestration/control layer of agentic AI systems—not the LLM weights themselves—should be governed by Bayesian decision theory for coherent decision-making under uncertainty.
- Proposes calibrated belief maintenance, Bayesian belief updating from agent/human interactions, and utility-aware action selection as practical design principles for agentic orchestrators.
- Relevance assessed for the configured profile.

**Motivation**  
Many high-value agentic deployments (tool selection, expert routing, resource allocation) require principled decisions under uncertainty, yet current LLM-based orchestrators lack formal probabilistic grounding. Making LLMs themselves Bayesian is computationally prohibitive; the authors seek a tractable alternative.

**Hypothesis**  
Bayesian consistency should be enforced at the orchestration/control layer of agentic systems rather than at the LLM parameter level, enabling coherent, calibrated, and utility-aware decision-making without requiring Bayesian LLM inference.

**Methodology**  
This is a position paper with no empirical experiments. The authors articulate theoretical properties for Bayesian control, draw on Bayesian decision theory, and provide concrete design patterns and examples illustrating how calibrated beliefs and utility-aware policies can be embedded in agentic orchestration loops.

**Results**  
No quantitative results are reported. The paper delivers a conceptual framework: (1) maintain beliefs over task-relevant latent quantities, (2) update beliefs from observed agentic and human-AI interactions, (3) select actions via utility-maximizing policies—all implemented at the orchestrator level above the LLM.

**Interpretation**  
The key insight is a clean architectural separation: LLMs handle language/reasoning while a Bayesian orchestrator handles uncertainty quantification and decision-making. This is a pragmatic middle ground between fully Bayesian LLMs (intractable) and purely heuristic orchestrators (incoherent).

**Context**  
Connects to active work on agent architectures, scalable oversight, and safe agent design. The KB contains related work on agent failure modes (TraceElephant, HINTBench, ClawsBench) that highlight exactly the kinds of orchestration failures this framework aims to address. Bayesian approaches to calibration and uncertainty are well-established in ML but underexplored in LLM agent orchestration.

**Limitations**  
As a position paper, claims are not empirically validated. The practical overhead of maintaining and updating explicit Bayesian belief states in real-time orchestration is not fully addressed. Scalability to complex, long-horizon tasks with high-dimensional latent spaces remains an open question.

**Why it matters to you**  
Moderately relevant to AI safety and agent architecture research. Bayesian orchestration could improve agent reliability, calibration, and controllability—all safety-adjacent concerns. However, it does not directly address alignment, adversarial robustness, or reward modeling.

### 3. [To Call or Not to Call: A Framework to Assess and Optimize LLM Tool Calling](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.00737)

**Score: 7/10** · Novelty: notable · Watchlist: Relevant to agent failure modes and tool-use safety; moderate priority for AI safety researchers studying agentic systems.  
*Authors: Qinyuan Wu, Soumi Das, Mahsa Amani, Arijit Nag, Seungeon Lee, Krishna P. Gummadi, Abhilasha Ravichander, Muhammad Bilal Zafar*  
*Categories: cs.AI*

**Triage:** 7

**TL;DR**
- Introduces a decision-theoretic framework to evaluate LLM web-search tool-calling decisions along three axes: necessity, utility, and affordability.
- Finds a systematic misalignment between models' self-perceived and true need/utility for tool calls.
- Trains lightweight hidden-state estimators that act as controllers to improve tool-call decisions across 3 tasks and 6 models.

**Motivation**  
Agentic LLMs frequently call external tools (especially web search) when it is unnecessary, redundant, or even harmful. There is no principled framework for deciding *when* a tool call is actually warranted, leaving models to rely on poorly calibrated self-assessment.

**Hypothesis**  
Tool-calling decisions can be decomposed into measurable factors (necessity, utility, affordability), and models' internal representations contain enough signal to train estimators that outperform the models' own self-perceived tool-use decisions.

**Methodology**  
Two analytical lenses are applied: a normative lens (optimal tool-call allocation as ground truth) and a descriptive lens (observed model behavior as proxy for self-perceived need/utility). Lightweight estimators are trained on models' hidden states to predict true need and utility, then used as simple controllers to gate tool calls. Evaluated across 3 tasks and 6 LLMs.

**Results**  
Models' self-perceived need and utility are frequently misaligned with their true need and utility. The hidden-state-based controllers consistently improve tool-call decision quality and downstream task performance compared to the default self-perceived setup across all tested models and tasks.

**Interpretation**  
LLMs are poorly calibrated about when to invoke tools—they neither reliably know what they don't know nor accurately predict whether a tool response will help. Probing internal representations is a more reliable signal than letting the model decide autonomously, suggesting a promising direction for lightweight, interpretability-adjacent agent control.

**Context**  
Sits at the intersection of agentic AI, retrieval-augmented generation, and model calibration. Related to work on adaptive RAG (when to retrieve), self-knowledge in LLMs, and agent failure mode analysis. The hidden-state probing approach echoes mechanistic interpretability methods applied to practical agent control.

**Limitations**  
Analysis is scoped to web search tools only; generalization to other tool types (code execution, APIs) is unverified. The lightweight estimators require access to model hidden states, limiting applicability to black-box API models. Evaluation breadth (3 tasks, 6 models) may not cover diverse real-world agentic scenarios.

**Why it matters to you**  
Directly relevant to agent architecture safety and failure modes—specifically the risk of over-reliance on or misuse of tools. The hidden-state controller approach is also tangentially relevant to interpretability-based oversight of agent behavior.
